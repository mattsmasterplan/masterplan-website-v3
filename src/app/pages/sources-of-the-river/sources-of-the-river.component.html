Notes from the text:

(pg. vii) Work that Thompson could have read while being educated:

Travels through the Interior Parts of North America (1778) by Jonathan Carver

Journey from Prince of Wales Fort (1795) by Samuel Hearne

(pg. 5) A biography called Narrative was written from Thompson's notes

(pg. 5) He was moving through territory that had never been described on paper

(pg. 10) 1784 Thompson sailed from London to Hudson Bay – Churchill Factory on western shore

(pg. 11) French trader controlled great lakes region but had British below in Ohio and above by Hudson Bay. Britain claimed the land but French didn’t acknowledge them. French and Indian wars broke out. France surrendered all claim in 1763.

(pg. 13) Bay Company profits were suffering when Thompson landed at Churchill Factory in 1784. Thompson described life at the factory as “growling at the cold” and “quarrelling with mosquitoes and sand flies.”

(pg. 14) 1785 Thompson reassigned to York Factory, 150 miles to the south

(pg. 15) line-hauling: attach long ropes to the bow of a canoe. Men ashore haul the boat upstream while one stays aboard to steer. Thompson labored for days at the end of a line.

(pg. 16) Traveling on Saskatchewan river, met French trader at a post. “Every sentence he spoke or answer he made was attended by a smile and a slight bow, our men grave and stiff as pokers. A scot trader was “happy to have the pleasure of speaking English”.

(pg. 16) 1786 Thompson helped build South Branch House

(pg. 19) Thompson described the Rocky Mountains as “an impassable barrier, even to the Eagle” when he saw them for the first time

(pg. 21) A Cree Indian told Thompson, about a stallion, “But as he was a slave to Man, like the dog, which carried our things; he was named Big Dog”

(pg. 23) The Blackfeet tribes were the first in their part of the country to own both horses and guns. They soon forced their rivals off the choicest buffalo grounds.

(pg. 24) Thompson describes bitter chokecherries: “Required the powers of an Ostrich to digest”

(pg. 25) In Narrative Thompson writes “The erect walk of the Indian is shown to great advantage….the white people seldom in an erect posture, their bodies swayed from right to left, and some with their arms, as if to saw a passage through the air”

(pg. 26) To the Blackfeet, the left hand was the hand of greeting, whereas the right hand that through weapons and pulled triggers was an insult. “If one of our people offer you his left hand, give him your left hand, for the right hand is no mark of friendship”

(pg. 36) Canadians and Indians inquired why Thompson observed the sun and moon. He explained “it was to determine the distance and direction from the place I observed to other places”. Thompson went on to write that neither the Indians or Canadians believed him.

(pg. 36) In summer of 1790, Thompson was tasked with charting the 750 mile route from Cumberland House to York Factory. These maps were sent to the Bay Company headquarters in London, and plotted onto official maps of the region by cartographers.

(pg. 36) Bay Company recognized Thompsons talents and sent him a gift of a brass sextant made by Dollond

(pg. 41) Thompson headed to Grand Portage, the North West Company’s field headquarters on Lake Superior

(pg. 42) Thompson left Grand Portage and arrived at Rainy Lake where he was to survey the region.

(pg. 49) The marriage customs of the Cree Indians are described by Thompson: “Their marriages are without noise or ceremony; nothing is requisite but the consent of the parties and the parents… when contrariety of disposition prevails, so that they cannot live peaceably together, they separate with as little ceremony as they came together, and both parties are free to attach themselves to whom they will”

(pg. 50) Fall 1800, Thompson and his wife Charlotte took up residence at the North West Company’s new Rocky Mountain House

(pg. 59) Thompson describes how the Piegan Indians hunt eagles for sport:

“The natives made shallow pits which they covered with slender willows and grass under which they lay, with a large piece of fresh meat opposite their breasts; thus arranged they patiently await the flight of the eagle, which is first seen very high, scaling in rude circles, but gradually lowering, till at length he seems determined to pounce upon the meat, his descent is then very swift with his claws extended, the moment he touches the meat the Indians grasp his two legs in his hands, and dashes him, through the slender willows to the bottom of the pit and strikes his head till he is dead… Lying in this position, and frequently somewhat benumbed, it requires an active man to pull down an Eagle with his wings expanded, and dash him to the ground… As the Eagle never loses his courage, the whole must be quickly done, or the Eagle will dart his beak into the mans face, and thus get away”

(pg. 60) A Piegan chief lectured his men on their rude behavior: “That it was an Act neither brave nor manly to rob a few Strangers”

(pg. 69) Describing his experience in the front range of the Rockies “The scene around us has nothing of the agreeable in it, All nature seems to frown, the mountains are dreary, rude & wild, beyond the power of the pencil”

(pg. 76) When Meriwether Lewis and William Clark started up the Missouri River in 1804, they carried a copy of the chart that David Thompson had made on his trip to the Mandans seven years earlier

(pg. 78) Blackfeet tribes made a sharp distinction between the Canadian traders, dubbed “Northern White Men”, and the American white men, whom they called “Big Knives”

(pg. 80) Kootenai Indians were delighted at the news of the furmens coming, and set off to work beaver

(pg. 81) In preparation to cross the Rockies, Thompson sent dogsleds up the frozen Saskatchewan as far as the first ridge of mountains. There the men cached over a thousand pounds of nonperishable supplies

(pg. 86) Thompson stopped to refresh his horses near Kootanae Pound, a narrow strip of grassland that led up to a sheer precipice. Kootenai hunters had long used this place as a buffalo jump, herding the animals up the slope and then driving them over the edge of the cliff

(pg. 97) Piegans joined the camp, and after a week they got hungry and left. They stole three horses on their way out.

(pg. 102) Columbia Lake, the source of the Columbia River, lies a few miles upstream from Windermere Lake in southeastern British Columbia. (one of these lakes) is bordered on the east by Mount Sabine and on the west by Purcell Range.

(pg. 103) Thompson had named a river McGillivrays River in honor of his bosses. Today it is known as Kootenay River in British Columbia or the Kootenai River in Montana and Idaho (spelling difference)

(pg. 111) A quote from a business letter Thompson wrote: “You know that I do nothing without calculation, so that I know fairly what I am about”

(pg. 125) Thompson noted: “Legs of the Lake Indians crooked inwards by sitting cross-legged in their small canoes”

(pg. 138) Thompson could be considered a philosopher. The usage of that term at that time was someone well versed in a particular area of natural science, such as botany, zoology, or in this case, astronomy.

(pg. 145) In 1809 Thompson reached the marshy plains around the modern town of Cusick, Washington. Friendly Kalispel Indians camped there were so completely untouched by white culture that they lacked iron tools of any sort.

(pg. 149) Thompson had ordered twenty pounds of soap and noted “For without soap, there is no effective cleanliness; this we know very well”

(pg. 154) Thompson referring to a stretch of land along the Flathead River: “…it will become the abode of civilized Man” speaking about its rich soil and opportunity for pastoral farming

(pg. 168) High tensions between the Britain and the United States caused tension in the region. None of these international crises affected Thompson’s plans.

(pg. 173) First week of December 1810, the temperature had not risen higher than twenty below zero for several days. Thompson was camped near Brule Lake.

(pg. 176) Thompson described an explorer who beat one of his dogs: “He is what we call a flash man, a showy fellow before the women but a coward in heart, and would willingly desert if he had the courage to go alone.”

(pg. 176) January 1811 Thompson crested Athabasca Pass

(pg. 183) The Colville River empties into the Columbia just below Kettle Falls

(pg. 184) An Indian count Salmon as they travel upstream, but does not kill them. A Colville Indian Salmon Chief announces that the prescribed number of salmon had ascended the falls, then men set out nets and baskets to begin the harvest in earnest.

(pg. 191) Thompson developed a method to count Indian groups he met. It was his custom to number the men who showed up to pass the pipe, then multiply by seven for an estimate of the size of the band, thus providing a consistent first census for the native peoples he encountered.

(pg. 195) The author is visiting Vernita Bridge over the Columbia River. He claims that this was once the hunting and root-digging territory of the Wanapam tribe; today it is mostly the domain of the U.S. Government. Downstream lies the Hanford Nuclear Reservation. To the east stretches an expansive national wildlife refuge. To the west the Yakima Firing Range occupies another large tract, set aside as a bombing range for the military.

(pg. 208) John Jacob Astor’s ship the Tonquin set sail from New York Harbor, bound for the Pacific via Cape Horn. In 1811 he reached the mouth of the Columbia. David Thompson was still more than a thousand miles upstream.

(pg. 214) Thompson reaches the mouth of the Columbia and finds Astor House. There is no thrill at the historic import of his arrival at the mouth of the Columbia, or any expression of disappointment that he did not get there first.

(pg. 215) The weather around the mouth of the Columbia is hardly ever fine; it is too powerful a place, too wild a conjunction among the peaks of the Cascades, the draining waters of the whole Inland Northwest, and the massive weather systems that spiral across the pacific.

(pg. 221) It is within memory of man that a huge landslide crashed down from Table Mountain into the heart of the Columbia Gorge.

(pg. 242) Thompson and his family got back in their canoe and dashed along the north shore of Lake Superior to the narrow straits that lead into Lake Huron.

(pg. 243) When Thompson arrived in Montreal in the fall of 1812, he walked down a city street for the first time in twenty-seven years. He purchased a house in the village of Terrebonne, north of the city.

(pg. 245) Thompsons big map of 1814 is on permanent display at the Provincial Archives of Ontario in Toronto. At ten by six-and-a-half feet, the chart covers an entire wall at the entrance of the archive’s reading room.

(pg. 246) Thompson developed special regard for the peak he named Mount Nelson, “which stands alone in native grandeur”. On his map he drew a side view of the peak. It’s the only construction like it on the whole chart, a nod to the mountain that stood at the center of all his western movements.

(pg. 249) Summer 1819, Thompson was working again as a surveyor. He was on the western end of Lake Erie, in an area of miasmic swamp.

(pg. 252) David and Charlotte Thompson had thirteen children.

(pg. 253) May 1837 found Thompson interviewing, at age sixty-seven, for the job of clarifying the Muskoka-Madawaska River route from Georgian Bay to the Ottawa River.

(pg. 254) Arrowsmith of London, a current world premier cartographer according to this author

(pg. 254) Britain and the United States were arguing about their border. Thompson once suggested a boundary along the 47th parallel from the Rocky Mountains to the Columbia, then down the center of that river to the sea.

(pg. 258) Thompson harnessed the wrong horse to hus buggy, wondering all the way why his steed was acting strangely. When everyone laughed at his mistake, “he enjoyed it himself and thought it a good joke.”

(pg. 258) Thompson died February 1857, two months before his eighty-seventh birthday. He is buried with his wife in the Mount Royal cemetery in Montreal.
