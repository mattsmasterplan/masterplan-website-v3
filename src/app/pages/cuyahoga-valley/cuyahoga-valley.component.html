Cuyahoga Valley

July 2018

Geographic Setting

Cuyahoga River, the natural central feature of Cuyahoga Valley, has provided great wealth to the area surrounding it. The economic development of Akron and Cleveland is in part thanks to the Ohio & Erie Canal built in 1827. The canal was built parallel to the river and consisted of many locks to allow boat passage. Access to this waterway led to high concentrations of urban and heavy industrialized areas along the river. Located in the Appalachian basin oil producing region, the valley is dotted with pumps extracting oil and natural gas.

The Cuyahoga River begins within 15 miles of the Lake Erie shore as two bubbling springs. The river first flows 60 miles to the southwest until it reaches near Akron. At this point, the river hits an east-west continental divide and turns sharply west, forming the bottom of a V shape. Cuyahoga River is a classic example of a meandering stream. Because of high flood potential, development of the Cuyahoga floodplain has been minimal. In 1969, the Cuyahoga became known as the “river that burned” because of fire fueled by pollution in the river. This fire was so severe that it spurred the passage of the Clean Water Act. The river is still not clean enough today for recreational activities.

The “Clinton” sandstone is an oil and gas deposit in the subsurface of eastern Ohio consisting of interbedded sandstones and shales. The formation has been producing oil and gas since 1887.

Geologic History

The earliest record of the geologic history in northeast Ohio is preserved in the igneous and metamorphic Precambrian basement rocks that underlie Paleozoic sedimentary rocks at a depth of over 5,000 feet in Cuyahoga Valley. These rocks are known in Ohio only from deep-well drilling and have been dated at more than one billion years old. Toward the end of the Silurian Period, seas gradually receded, and evaporation of seawater formed thick salt beds. These salt beds are actively mined 1600 feet below Lake Erie near Cleveland. There were at least four major continental ice age periods. From oldest to youngest, they are: Nebraskan, Kansan, Illinoian, and Wisconsinan. The Wisconsinan glacial drift deposits cover nearly all the bedrock in Cuyahoga Valley.

Geologic Features

Cuyahoga Valley National Park is located on the glaciated portion of the Allegheny Plateau, a physiographic region in the northwestern part of the larger Appalachian Plateaus Province. The Dover River flowed north, carving a deep valley into the Allegheny Plateau. This is now the Cuyahoga Valley. Bedrock exposed in the valley, as well as throughout Ohio, consists of relatively flat-lying, layered Paleozoic sedimentary rocks. Bedrock records events during three major periods.

The Devonian system is represented by dark gray to black shales (Ohio Shale) that accumulated in a tropical, stagnant sea. The Mississippian system is represented by a sequence of shale and interbedded siltstone (Bedford Shale), overlain by sandstone (Berea Sandstone), and silty, clayey, sandy shales (Cuyahoga Formation). This sequence was deposited in changing environments from offshore marine, to coastal, to fluvial, and back to offshore marine. The Pennsylvanian system is represented by sandstone and conglomerate (Sharon Conglomerate) deposited by fluvial braided streams.

Berea Sandstone is the most recent rock layer, and it is covered with glacial till. Exposed bedrock at quarrying sites reveals glacial striations. Pink granites and banded gneiss are conspicuous erratic compositions and seen frequently in streambeds. These igneous and metamorphic rocks have been transported from the Precambrian Canadian Shield to the Cuyahoga valley by immense glaciers. As glacial ice melted in the valley, a series of ephemeral lakes were formed as meltwaters were trapped between the receding ice and end moraines. The Cuyahoga Valley is a classic example of a buried glacial valley. A valley that has been partially filled in with glacial drift. The pre-glacial valley floor is now about 500 feet below the present valley floor.

The most abundant subsurface data available for the “Clinton” comes from the more than 80,000 wells drilled over the years. Well data include the depths at which the “Clinton” was encountered while drilling from the surface. Drillers record the depths at which they penetrate the tops of distinctive formations.

My Visit

Cuyahoga Valley was only three hours away, making it the closest national park to me. With my bike on the roof of my car, I made the trip. I parked on the north side of the park and rode my bike south along the towpath trail for 13 miles, stopping at the Boston general store for a break. The store is a national park visitors center, and it has exhibits showing the history of Cuyahoga Valley. Riding along the towpath trail, I saw carved stone structures every so often. These used to be locks that raised or lowered the water level for boats traveling along the canal. The canal was built along the river and much of it was now dried out. I realized why the trail I was riding was called the towpath. Riding along the Cuyahoga river and seeing the history made me understand why the valley was declared a national park. As I was riding back, there was a National Park Service volunteer standing along the trail with binoculars setup. He allowed me to look and I could see a Peregrine falcon. These birds can dive at 220+ MPH but this one was just resting on a concrete ledge. When I made it back to my car, I had ridden almost 26 miles. Cuyahoga Valley did not dissapoint when it comes to peaceful scenic nature. Finally, I found something I like about Ohio.
