Great Basin

Visited in August 2018

Geology

A technique I use to understand the geology of an area is to look at oil well summaries. Drillers record the rock layers they pierce and at what depth, and many states make this information available online. Great Basin has no recorded wells inside the park boundries, but clusters of wells nearby tap into the Chainman Shale formation.

Map showing available well summaries and their location in comparison to Great Basin National Park.

Detailed drilling report from a nearly 12,000' well in the Great Basin region.

In 2014 the National Park Service released an incredibly detailed Geologic Resources Report for Great Basin. Pages 40 through 46 show excellent illustrations of how the North American continent appeared during different geologic periods. The Great Basin region was repeatedly covered by shallow seas until it began to dry out 245 million years ago.

My Visit

Driving from Salt Lake City to Great Basin was my first time driving on long and straight desert roads. It took nearly 4 hours but I drove up to the Snake Range breaking through the flat desert landscape. I had booked a tour of Lehman Cave. These group tours move kind of slow, but the information presented by the guide was interesting. It was emphasized that Lehman Cave has a high concentration of a rare formation called a cave shield. The guide pointed them out as we went along, some more impressive than others. I was surprised to see a lot of historic writing on the cave walls from the early explorers. My favorite part of the cave was an exposed rock slab showing bright layers. Great Basin had hiking trails and mountain roads to explore, but I was on limited time for this visit and had a long drive back to SLC.
